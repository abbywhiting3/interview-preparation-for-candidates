The STAR Method

A proven framework for answering behavioral interview questions with clarity and impact.

S

Situation

Set the scene. Describe the context or challenge you faced in a prior role.

T

Task

Explain your specific responsibility or objective in that situation.

A

Action

Detail the steps you personally took to address the challenge.

R

Result

Share the outcome. Quantify it if possible and explain what you learned.

Example: Attorney Behavioral Answer Using STAR

S - Situation: At my previous firm, a high-value client threatened to leave after a billing dispute escalated during a complex commercial litigation matter.
T - Task: As the associate managing the account, I needed to resolve the dispute, retain the client, and ensure ongoing case work was not disrupted.
A - Action: I initiated a meeting with the client to review every line item, prepared a revised billing summary, and coordinated with the partner to offer a fair adjustment. I also created a go-forward billing communication protocol.
R - Result: The client stayed with the firm, approved the revised invoice within 48 hours, and later expanded our engagement to include two additional matters worth over $200K in annual revenue.

Common Interview Questions

Select a position type to see typical questions and expert guidance on how to answer them.

Why did you choose to practice law in this specific area? ▼

Connect your answer to a genuine interest or formative experience. Reference specific cases, coursework, or mentors that drew you to the practice area.

Tip: Avoid generic answers. Mention a specific moment or experience that ignited your passion for this area of law.
How do you manage a heavy caseload and competing deadlines? ▼

Describe your organizational system: calendaring tools, task prioritization, and how you communicate with partners about bandwidth. Give a concrete example.

Tip: Mention specific tools like practice management software or docketing systems you use to stay organized.
Tell me about a difficult opposing counsel situation and how you handled it. ▼

Use the STAR method. Focus on professionalism, de-escalation, and keeping the client's best interest front and center.

Tip: Never badmouth opposing counsel. Frame the difficulty around the situation, not the person.
What is your approach to client development and business generation? ▼

Discuss networking, industry involvement, writing or speaking engagements, and maintaining referral relationships. If early-career, discuss your plan.

Tip: Even junior attorneys should show awareness of business development through bar associations, CLEs, or community involvement.
Describe a time you made an error on a case. What happened? ▼

Be honest but strategic. Choose a real but recoverable mistake. Walk through what happened, how you addressed it, and safeguards you put in place.

Tip: Firms value self-awareness and accountability. Showing you can own a mistake and improve from it builds trust.
Why are you looking to leave your current firm? ▼

Frame your answer around what you are moving toward, not what you are running from. Talk about growth, practice area alignment, or culture fit.

Tip: Never speak negatively about your current employer. Keep the focus on the opportunity ahead.
What is your experience defending insurance carriers and their insureds? ▼

Detail the types of carriers you have worked with, the lines of coverage (auto, CGL, professional liability, homeowners), and your role in the litigation lifecycle from initial coverage analysis through trial.

Tip: Name specific carrier clients if your current firm allows it. Hiring partners want to gauge whether your experience aligns with their book.
How do you handle billing guidelines and litigation management requirements from carriers? ▼

Discuss your familiarity with carrier billing guidelines, rate caps, task-based billing codes, and litigation plans. Explain how you manage budgets while still providing thorough representation.

Tip: Show you understand the business side. Carriers care about cost-efficient defense, not just legal wins.
Describe your experience with coverage analysis and reservation of rights letters. ▼

Walk through your process for reviewing a policy, identifying coverage issues, and communicating with the carrier. Mention whether you have drafted reservation of rights or denial letters.

Tip: If you have experience with both coverage opinions and defense work, highlight that dual perspective as a strength.
How do you evaluate a case for early resolution vs. taking it to trial? ▼

Explain your framework: liability exposure, damages assessment, litigation costs, policy limits, and the insured's appetite for risk. Discuss how you present settlement recommendations to the carrier.

Tip: Demonstrate that you think strategically about total cost of defense, not just win/loss outcomes.
Tell me about your deposition and trial experience in personal injury defense. ▼

Quantify your experience: number of depositions taken and defended, mediations attended, trials (bench and jury). Describe your approach to preparing witnesses and cross-examining plaintiffs and experts.

Tip: Be specific with numbers. Saying you have taken 50+ depositions is much more impactful than saying you have significant deposition experience.
What types of real estate transactions have you handled? ▼

Break down your experience by transaction type: commercial acquisitions and dispositions, leasing, financing, development, and residential closings. Mention deal sizes and complexity levels.

Tip: Quantify your deals. Saying you have closed $50M+ in commercial transactions carries weight.
How do you approach due diligence on a commercial real estate deal? ▼

Walk through your checklist: title review, survey analysis, environmental reports, zoning compliance, lease abstracts, estoppels, and entity formation. Explain how you coordinate with the deal team.

Tip: Show that you are systematic and thorough. Missed issues in due diligence can kill deals or create liability.
Describe a deal that fell apart or hit a major obstacle. How did you handle it? ▼

Use STAR. Describe the issue (title defect, environmental problem, financing failure), your role in identifying solutions, and the outcome. Show creative problem-solving.

Tip: Real estate partners want attorneys who find solutions, not just flag problems. Show how you helped save or restructure the deal.
What is your experience with commercial lease negotiations? ▼

Describe whether you have represented landlords, tenants, or both. Discuss key lease provisions you negotiate: rent escalations, CAM charges, assignment and subletting, options to renew, and tenant improvement allowances.

Tip: If you have represented both sides, highlight how that gives you a strategic advantage in negotiations.
Do you primarily represent claimants, employers, or carriers? ▼

Clearly state your side and explain your typical caseload. If you have experience on both sides, discuss how that gives you insight into opposing strategies. Mention case volume and types of injuries handled.

Tip: Workers' comp firms are often side-specific. Be clear and honest about which side you have more experience on.
How do you handle a disputed claim where the employer denies the injury is work-related? ▼

Describe your approach to gathering evidence: medical records, witness statements, job descriptions, surveillance review, and IME coordination. Explain how you build the record for hearing.

Tip: Show your knowledge of the administrative process and how you prepare for contested hearings before the workers' comp commission or board.
What is your experience with Medicare Set-Asides and settlement structures? ▼

Discuss whether you have handled MSA allocations, CMS submissions, and structured settlements. Explain your approach to protecting the client while ensuring compliance with federal requirements.

Tip: MSA knowledge is increasingly valuable. If you have direct CMS submission experience, highlight it.
Describe your hearing and trial experience in workers' comp cases. ▼

Quantify your appearances before the workers' comp board or commission. Describe the types of hearings: temporary total disability, permanent partial, vocational rehabilitation disputes, and settlement approvals.

Tip: Workers' comp is a volume practice. Demonstrating a high number of hearings shows you can handle the pace.
How do you handle high-conflict custody disputes? ▼

Discuss your approach to managing emotionally charged clients, working with guardians ad litem, presenting evidence on best interests of the child, and navigating contested custody evaluations.

Tip: Demonstrate emotional intelligence. Family law firms want attorneys who can manage client emotions while staying focused on legal strategy.
What is your experience with complex asset division in divorce cases? ▼

Detail your experience with business valuations, retirement account division (QDROs), real property, stock options, and hidden asset investigations. Mention forensic accountant collaboration.

Tip: High-net-worth divorce work requires financial literacy. Show you can work with numbers, not just legal arguments.
How do you approach settlement negotiations in family law matters? ▼

Explain your mediation experience, collaborative law training if applicable, and how you prepare clients for realistic outcomes. Discuss how you balance advocacy with the benefits of settlement.

Tip: Courts and clients increasingly favor settlement. Show that you are a skilled negotiator, not just a litigator.
Describe a case where you had to seek or defend against an order of protection. ▼

Walk through the process: emergency petitions, evidentiary hearings, witness preparation, and the impact on related custody or divorce proceedings. Emphasize sensitivity and urgency.

Tip: These cases involve safety concerns. Show that you take them seriously and can act quickly under pressure.
What types of transactions have you worked on and what was your role? ▼

Detail your M&A, private equity, venture capital, or general corporate experience. Specify whether you ran deals or supported as part of a larger team. Mention deal sizes and industries.

Tip: Be clear about your level of responsibility. Did you draft the purchase agreement or just review ancillary documents? Honesty here builds credibility.
How do you manage multiple closings happening simultaneously? ▼

Describe your project management approach: closing checklists, signature pages, escrow coordination, and how you communicate with all parties. Show that you thrive in high-pressure, deadline-driven environments.

Tip: Transactional work is all about execution. Show that you are organized, detail-oriented, and calm under pressure.
Walk me through how you would draft or negotiate a key provision in a purchase agreement. ▼

Pick a provision you know well (indemnification, reps and warranties, earnouts, non-competes) and walk through your approach: understanding the business terms, identifying risk allocation, and negotiating with opposing counsel.

Tip: This tests your technical depth. Pick something you have actually negotiated and can speak to with confidence.
How do you stay current on regulatory changes affecting your corporate clients? ▼

Mention specific resources: SEC updates, state corporate law developments, industry publications, and continuing education. Show that you proactively advise clients on changes that affect their business.

Tip: Corporate clients expect their attorneys to be business advisors, not just document drafters. Show a broader perspective.
What types of cases have you supported and what was your role? ▼

Walk through your experience by practice area. Describe specific tasks: drafting discovery, managing document review, coordinating depositions, filing motions, or maintaining case calendars.

Tip: Be specific about volume and complexity. Saying you managed discovery for a 50,000-document production is more impactful than just saying you did document review.
How do you prioritize tasks when multiple attorneys need your help at the same time? ▼

Explain your communication approach: checking deadlines, asking about court dates vs. internal deadlines, and keeping attorneys informed about your bandwidth.

Tip: Show that you are proactive about managing expectations rather than just reactive.
What legal software and technology are you proficient in? ▼

List platforms: Westlaw, LexisNexis, Relativity, NetDocuments, Clio, iManage, etc. Mention e-filing systems and any certifications.

Tip: If you lack experience in a specific tool, emphasize your ability to learn quickly with a concrete example.
Describe a time you caught an error that could have affected a case outcome. ▼

Use STAR. Describe what you found, the potential impact, how you flagged it, and the result. This demonstrates attention to detail and the value you bring to a legal team.

Tip: Paralegals are often the last line of defense on details. This story should show you take that responsibility seriously.
Walk me through your experience with regulatory compliance. ▼

Discuss specific regulations you have worked with (SEC, FINRA, SOX, AML/KYC). Describe your role in audits, compliance reporting, or policy implementation.

Tip: Reference specific frameworks and regulations by name to demonstrate depth of knowledge.
How do you approach risk assessment in financial transactions? ▼

Explain your analytical process: data review, stakeholder consultation, scenario modeling, and communicating findings to decision-makers.

Tip: Show that you balance thoroughness with business practicality. Firms want risk-aware professionals, not risk-averse ones.
Describe a time you identified a discrepancy in financial data. ▼

Use STAR. Describe what you found, the potential impact, how you investigated and resolved it, and what controls you recommended.

Tip: Attention to detail is critical in finance. This question is your chance to prove it with a real story.
How do you stay current with changes in financial regulations and market conditions? ▼

Mention industry publications, professional associations, continuing education, and how you translate regulatory changes into actionable guidance for your team or clients.

Tip: Show that you are a self-directed learner who does not wait to be told about changes that affect your work.

Quick Tips

Last-minute reminders to help you walk in with confidence.

🔍

Research the Employer

Know the firm's practice areas, recent wins, culture, and leadership. Tailor your answers to show you understand their world.

👔

Dress the Part

When in doubt, overdress. Business professional is the standard for legal and financial services interviews.

✍

Prepare Your Questions

Have 3-5 thoughtful questions ready. Ask about team structure, growth opportunities, and what success looks like in the role.

💬

Practice Out Loud

Rehearse your STAR stories and key talking points. Saying them out loud builds muscle memory and reduces anxiety.

⏰

Arrive Early

Plan to arrive 10-15 minutes early. For virtual interviews, test your tech setup at least 30 minutes before.

✉

Follow Up

Send a personalized thank-you email within 24 hours. Reference something specific from your conversation.
