The STAR Method

A proven framework for answering behavioral interview questions with clarity and impact.

S

Situation

Set the scene. Describe the context or challenge you faced in a prior role.

T

Task

Explain your specific responsibility or objective in that situation.

A

Action

Detail the steps you personally took to address the challenge.

R

Result

Share the outcome. Quantify it if possible and explain what you learned.

Example: Attorney Behavioral Answer Using STAR

S - Situation: At my previous firm, a high-value client threatened to leave after a billing dispute escalated during a complex commercial litigation matter.
T - Task: As the associate managing the account, I needed to resolve the dispute, retain the client, and ensure ongoing case work was not disrupted.
A - Action: I initiated a meeting with the client to review every line item, prepared a revised billing summary, and coordinated with the partner to offer a fair adjustment. I also created a go-forward billing communication protocol.
R - Result: The client stayed with the firm, approved the revised invoice within 48 hours, and later expanded our engagement to include two additional matters worth over $200K in annual revenue.

Common Interview Questions

Select a position type to see typical questions and expert guidance on how to answer them.

Why did you choose to practice law in this specific area? ▼

Connect your answer to a genuine interest or formative experience. Reference specific cases, coursework, or mentors that drew you to the practice area. Show that your choice is intentional, not random.

Tip: Avoid generic answers. Mention a specific moment, case, or experience that ignited your passion for this area of law.
How do you manage a heavy caseload and competing deadlines? ▼

Describe your organizational system: calendaring tools, task prioritization methods, and how you communicate with partners about bandwidth. Give a concrete example of a time you juggled multiple urgent matters.

Tip: Mention specific tools (e.g., practice management software, docketing systems) and frameworks you use to stay organized.
Tell me about a difficult opposing counsel situation and how you handled it. ▼

Use the STAR method here. Focus on professionalism, your ability to de-escalate tension, and how you kept the client's best interest front and center. Emphasize the resolution and what you learned.

Tip: Never badmouth opposing counsel. Frame the difficulty around the situation, not the person.
What is your approach to client development and business generation? ▼

Discuss networking habits, industry involvement, writing or speaking engagements, and how you maintain relationships with referral sources. If you are early-career, discuss your plan and any client-facing experience you do have.

Tip: Even junior attorneys should show awareness of business development. Mention bar associations, CLEs, or community involvement.
Describe a time you made an error on a case. What happened? ▼

Be honest but strategic. Choose a real but recoverable mistake. Walk through what happened, how you caught or addressed it, what you communicated to the team, and the safeguards you put in place afterward.

Tip: Firms value self-awareness and accountability. Showing you can own a mistake and improve from it builds trust.
Why are you looking to leave your current firm? ▼

Frame your answer around what you are moving toward, not what you are running from. Talk about growth opportunities, practice area alignment, culture fit, or platform advantages at the target firm.

Tip: Never speak negatively about your current employer. Keep the focus on the opportunity ahead.
What types of cases have you supported and what was your role? ▼

Walk through your experience by practice area. Describe specific tasks: drafting discovery, managing document review, coordinating depositions, filing motions, or maintaining case calendars.

Tip: Be specific about volume and complexity. Saying you managed discovery for a 50,000-document production is more impactful than just saying you did document review.
How do you prioritize tasks when multiple attorneys need your help at the same time? ▼

Explain your communication approach: checking deadlines, asking about court dates vs. internal deadlines, and keeping attorneys informed about your bandwidth. Give a real example.

Tip: Show that you are proactive about managing expectations rather than just reactive.
What legal software and technology are you proficient in? ▼

List platforms you have used: Westlaw, LexisNexis, Relativity, NetDocuments, Clio, iManage, etc. Mention your comfort level with e-filing systems and any certifications or training.

Tip: If you lack experience in a specific tool they use, emphasize your ability to learn quickly and give an example of picking up new software fast.
Walk me through your experience with regulatory compliance. ▼

Discuss specific regulations you have worked with (SEC, FINRA, SOX, AML/KYC). Describe your role in audits, compliance reporting, or policy implementation. Quantify scope where possible.

Tip: Reference specific frameworks and regulations by name to demonstrate depth of knowledge.
How do you approach risk assessment in financial transactions? ▼

Explain your analytical process: data review, stakeholder consultation, scenario modeling, and how you communicate findings to decision-makers. Provide a concrete example of identifying and mitigating a risk.

Tip: Show that you balance thoroughness with business practicality. Firms want risk-aware professionals, not risk-averse ones.
Describe a time you identified a discrepancy in financial data. ▼

Use the STAR method. Describe what you found, the potential impact, how you investigated and resolved it, and what controls you recommended to prevent recurrence.

Tip: Attention to detail is critical in finance. This question is your chance to prove it with a real story.

Quick Tips

Last-minute reminders to help you walk in with confidence.

🔍

Research the Employer

Know the firm's practice areas, recent wins, culture, and leadership. Tailor your answers to show you understand their world.

👔

Dress the Part

When in doubt, overdress. Business professional is the standard for legal and financial services interviews.

✍

Prepare Your Questions

Have 3-5 thoughtful questions ready. Ask about team structure, growth opportunities, and what success looks like in the role.

💬

Practice Out Loud

Rehearse your STAR stories and key talking points. Saying them out loud builds muscle memory and reduces anxiety.

⏰

Arrive Early

Plan to arrive 10-15 minutes early. For virtual interviews, test your tech setup at least 30 minutes before.

✉

Follow Up

Send a personalized thank-you email within 24 hours. Reference something specific from your conversation.
