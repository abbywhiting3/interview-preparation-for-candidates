General Attorney

Why did you choose this practice area? ▼

Connect to a genuine interest or formative experience. Reference specific cases, coursework, or mentors.

Tip: Mention a specific moment that ignited your passion.
How do you manage a heavy caseload? ▼

Describe calendaring tools, task prioritization, and communication with partners about bandwidth.

Tip: Name specific tools like practice management software.
Tell me about a difficult opposing counsel situation. ▼

Use STAR. Focus on professionalism and de-escalation.

Tip: Never badmouth opposing counsel.
What is your approach to business development? ▼

Discuss networking, industry involvement, writing, and referral relationships.

Tip: Even juniors should show BD awareness.
Describe a time you made an error on a case. ▼

Choose a recoverable mistake. Walk through what happened and safeguards you added.

Tip: Firms value accountability.
Why are you looking to leave your current firm? ▼

Frame around what you are moving toward: growth, alignment, culture.

Tip: Never speak negatively about your current employer.

Insurance Defense

What is your experience defending carriers and insureds? ▼

Detail carrier types, coverage lines, and your litigation lifecycle role.

Tip: Name carrier clients if permitted.
How do you handle carrier billing guidelines? ▼

Discuss rate caps, task-based billing, and budget management.

Tip: Show you understand cost-efficient defense.
Describe your coverage analysis experience. ▼

Walk through policy review, coverage issues, and ROR letters.

Tip: Dual coverage/defense perspective is a strength.
How do you evaluate early resolution vs. trial? ▼

Explain liability exposure, damages, costs, limits, and risk appetite analysis.

Tip: Think total cost of defense, not just win/loss.
How do you manage the tripartite relationship between carrier, insured, and counsel? ▼

Explain how you keep the insured informed, manage conflicts between insured and carrier interests, and maintain ethical duties to both parties throughout the litigation.

Tip: Mention Cumis counsel situations if you have that experience — it shows sophisticated understanding of insurance defense ethics.
What is your trial experience in insurance defense matters? ▼

Describe the number and types of trials, your role as lead or second chair, jurisdictions, and outcomes. Include arbitration and deposition experience as well.

Tip: If trial experience is limited, highlight strong motion practice and deposition skills as complementary strengths.
How do you stay current with changes in insurance law and coverage trends? ▼

Reference DRI, FDCC, state defense bar publications, Westlaw alerts, and internal firm updates. Mention any articles or presentations you have produced on emerging issues.

Tip: Carriers want lawyers who proactively educate them on emerging risks, not just react to claims as they come in.

Real Estate Attorney

What types of real estate transactions have you handled? ▼

Break down by type and deal size: acquisitions, leasing, financing, development.

Tip: Quantify. $50M+ carries weight.
How do you approach due diligence? ▼

Title, survey, environmental, zoning, lease abstracts, estoppels.

Tip: Show systematic thoroughness.
Describe a deal that hit a major obstacle. ▼

Use STAR. Show creative problem-solving.

Tip: Partners want solution-finders.
How do you handle title issues and survey defects discovered during closing? ▼

Walk through your process for clearing title exceptions: negotiating endorsements, obtaining gap indemnities, resolving survey defects, and working with title companies under tight timelines.

Tip: Show you can problem-solve at the closing table. Deals fall apart over title issues — firms want attorneys who can clear them fast.
Describe your experience with commercial lease negotiations. ▼

Discuss landlord vs. tenant representation, key deal points (rent abatement, TI allowances, exclusivity clauses, termination rights), and how you balance leverage with deal preservation.

Tip: Mention lease types: NNN, gross, ground leases. Breadth across asset classes (office, retail, industrial) is a differentiator.
How do you advise clients on zoning, land use, and entitlement matters? ▼

Describe your experience with variance applications, conditional use permits, rezoning petitions, and working with municipalities. Explain how you manage client expectations through long approval timelines.

Tip: Entitlement experience is highly valued in development-focused practices. Show familiarity with local planning boards and approval processes.
What is your experience with real estate finance and secured lending transactions? ▼

Discuss construction loans, permanent financing, mezzanine debt, CMBS loans, and intercreditor agreements. Describe your role on lender-side vs. borrower-side deals and how you manage complex multi-party closings.

Tip: Lender-side experience is particularly marketable. If you have both sides, say so — it shows you understand the full transaction picture.

How do you manage independent medical examinations and medical evidence? ▼

Describe how you select and prepare IME physicians, challenge treating physician opinions, use vocational experts, and handle medical records review to build or defend against permanent disability claims.

Tip: Apportionment arguments can dramatically reduce employer/carrier exposure. Show you actively pursue them rather than accepting claimant ratings at face value.
How do you handle subrogation and third-party claims in workers' comp matters? ▼

Describe how you identify third-party liability (products, premises, motor vehicle), coordinate with civil litigators, assert employer/carrier liens, and maximize recovery to offset claim costs.

Tip: Subrogation recovery directly reduces net claim costs. Employers and carriers value attorneys who proactively identify and pursue these opportunities.
How do you advise employers on return-to-work programs and light duty compliance? ▼

Explain how you help employers structure modified duty offers, ensure FMLA/ADA compliance when injuries overlap, document good-faith efforts to accommodate, and defend against retaliation claims.

Tip: Return-to-work programs reduce indemnity costs substantially. Advising proactively on compliance, not just litigating claims, makes you far more valuable to employer clients.

The STAR Method

A proven framework for answering behavioral interview questions with clarity and impact.

S

Situation

Set the scene. Describe the context or challenge you faced.

T

Task

Explain your specific responsibility or objective.

A

Action

Detail the steps you personally took.

R

Result

Share the outcome. Quantify it if possible.

Example: Attorney Using STAR

S: A high-value client threatened to leave after a billing dispute during complex commercial litigation.
T: As the associate managing the account, I needed to resolve the dispute and retain the client.
A: I reviewed every line item with the client, prepared a revised summary, coordinated with the partner on a fair adjustment, and created a billing communication protocol.
R: The client stayed, approved the invoice within 48 hours, and expanded our engagement by two matters worth $200K+ annually.

Interview Tips

Actionable advice to help you walk in prepared and walk out confident.

🔍

Research the Employer

Know the firm's practice areas, recent wins, key clients, and culture. Check their website, LinkedIn, and recent news before your interview.

👔

Dress Professionally

Business professional is the standard for legal, financial, and engineering interviews. When in doubt, overdress. First impressions are formed in seconds.

💬

Prepare Your Stories

Have 5-7 STAR stories ready that highlight leadership, problem-solving, teamwork, and handling adversity. Most behavioral questions fit one of these themes.

✍

Prepare Smart Questions

Have 3-5 thoughtful questions about team structure, growth opportunities, what success looks like in 90 days, and the firm's direction. Avoid asking about salary first.

🌎

Know Your Resume Cold

Be ready to speak fluently about every bullet on your resume. Interviewers follow up on specifics. Vague answers raise red flags.

⏰

Arrive Early

10-15 minutes early in person. For virtual interviews, test your audio, video, and internet 30 minutes before. Have a backup plan if tech fails.

🤔

Read the Room

Mirror the interviewer's energy. If they are formal, stay formal. If they are conversational, relax slightly. Strong emotional intelligence sets candidates apart.

📈

Quantify Everything

Whenever possible, use numbers. “Managed a caseload of 80+ active matters” is far stronger than “handled many cases.” Numbers make stories memorable and credible.

📢

Follow Up Within 24 Hours

Send a personalized thank-you email to each interviewer. Reference something specific from the conversation. It reinforces your interest and professionalism.

🌟

Show Genuine Enthusiasm

Firms hire people who want to be there. Express specific excitement about the role, the team, or the firm's work. Enthusiasm is contagious and memorable.

🚫

Avoid These Mistakes

Never speak negatively about former employers. Never check your phone. Never give one-word answers. Never lie about your experience — it always comes out.

🤝

Negotiate Thoughtfully

When an offer comes, take 24-48 hours before responding. Research market rates. Counter professionally and with a rationale — most firms expect it.
