General Attorney

Why did you choose this practice area? ▼

Connect to a genuine interest or formative experience. Reference specific cases, coursework, or mentors.

Tip: Mention a specific moment that ignited your passion.
How do you manage a heavy caseload? ▼

Describe calendaring tools, task prioritization, and communication with partners about bandwidth.

Tip: Name specific tools like practice management software.
Tell me about a difficult opposing counsel situation. ▼

Use STAR. Focus on professionalism and de-escalation.

Tip: Never badmouth opposing counsel.
What is your approach to business development? ▼

Discuss networking, industry involvement, writing, and referral relationships.

Tip: Even juniors should show BD awareness.
Describe a time you made an error on a case. ▼

Choose a recoverable mistake. Walk through what happened and safeguards you added.

Tip: Firms value accountability.
Why are you looking to leave your current firm? ▼

Frame around what you are moving toward: growth, alignment, culture.

Tip: Never speak negatively about your current employer.

Insurance Defense

What is your experience defending carriers and insureds? ▼

Detail carrier types, coverage lines, and your litigation lifecycle role.

Tip: Name carrier clients if permitted.
How do you handle carrier billing guidelines? ▼

Discuss rate caps, task-based billing, and budget management.

Tip: Show you understand cost-efficient defense.
Describe your coverage analysis experience. ▼

Walk through policy review, coverage issues, and ROR letters.

Tip: Dual coverage/defense perspective is a strength.
How do you evaluate early resolution vs. trial? ▼

Explain liability exposure, damages, costs, limits, and risk appetite analysis.

Tip: Think total cost of defense, not just win/loss.
How do you manage the tripartite relationship between carrier, insured, and counsel? ▼

Explain how you keep the insured informed, manage conflicts between insured and carrier interests, and maintain ethical duties to both parties throughout the litigation.

Tip: Mention Cumis counsel situations if you have that experience — it shows sophisticated understanding of insurance defense ethics.
What is your trial experience in insurance defense matters? ▼

Describe the number and types of trials, your role as lead or second chair, jurisdictions, and outcomes. Include arbitration and deposition experience as well.

Tip: If trial experience is limited, highlight strong motion practice and deposition skills as complementary strengths.
How do you stay current with changes in insurance law and coverage trends? ▼

Reference DRI, FDCC, state defense bar publications, Westlaw alerts, and internal firm updates. Mention any articles or presentations you have produced on emerging issues.

Tip: Carriers want lawyers who proactively educate them on emerging risks, not just react to claims as they come in.

Real Estate Attorney

What types of real estate transactions have you handled? ▼

Break down by type and deal size: acquisitions, leasing, financing, development.

Tip: Quantify. $50M+ carries weight.
How do you approach due diligence? ▼

Title, survey, environmental, zoning, lease abstracts, estoppels.

Tip: Show systematic thoroughness.
Describe a deal that hit a major obstacle. ▼

Use STAR. Show creative problem-solving.

Tip: Partners want solution-finders.
How do you handle title issues and survey defects discovered during closing? ▼

Walk through your process for clearing title exceptions: negotiating endorsements, obtaining gap indemnities, resolving survey defects, and working with title companies under tight timelines.

Tip: Show you can problem-solve at the closing table.
Describe your experience with commercial lease negotiations. ▼

Discuss landlord vs. tenant representation, key deal points (rent abatement, TI allowances, exclusivity clauses, termination rights), and how you balance leverage with deal preservation.

Tip: Mention lease types: NNN, gross, ground leases.
How do you advise clients on zoning, land use, and entitlement matters? ▼

Describe your experience with variance applications, conditional use permits, rezoning petitions, and working with municipalities.

Tip: Entitlement experience is highly valued in development-focused practices.
What is your experience with real estate finance and secured lending transactions? ▼

Discuss construction loans, permanent financing, mezzanine debt, CMBS loans, and intercreditor agreements.

Tip: Lender-side experience is particularly marketable.

Workers' Comp

Do you represent claimants, employers, or carriers? ▼

State your side clearly. Mention caseload volume and injury types.

Tip: Workers' comp firms are side-specific.
How do you handle disputed claims? ▼

Evidence gathering: medical records, witness statements, IME coordination.

Tip: Show administrative hearing knowledge.
What is your MSA and settlement experience? ▼

Discuss MSA allocations, CMS submissions, and structured settlements.

Tip: MSA knowledge is increasingly valuable.
How do you manage independent medical examinations and medical evidence? ▼

Describe how you select and prepare IME physicians, challenge treating physician opinions, use vocational experts, and handle medical records review.

Tip: Strong IME management can change the value of a case significantly.
Describe your experience with permanent disability ratings and apportionment. ▼

Explain how you use AMA Guides or state-specific rating systems, challenge inflated permanent disability ratings, and argue apportionment to pre-existing conditions.

Tip: Apportionment arguments can dramatically reduce employer/carrier exposure.
How do you handle subrogation and third-party claims in workers' comp matters? ▼

Describe how you identify third-party liability, coordinate with civil litigators, assert employer/carrier liens, and maximize recovery.

Tip: Subrogation recovery directly reduces net claim costs.
How do you advise employers on return-to-work programs and light duty compliance? ▼

Explain how you help employers structure modified duty offers, ensure FMLA/ADA compliance, and defend against retaliation claims.

Tip: Return-to-work programs reduce indemnity costs substantially.

Family Law

How do you handle high-conflict custody disputes? ▼

Discuss managing emotional clients, GALs, and best-interests evidence.

Tip: Demonstrate emotional intelligence.
What is your experience with complex asset division? ▼

Business valuations, QDROs, stock options, forensic accountants.

Tip: Show financial literacy.
How do you approach settlement negotiations? ▼

Mediation experience, collaborative law, realistic outcome preparation.

Tip: Courts favor settlement. Show negotiation skill.
How do you handle domestic violence or restraining order situations? ▼

Describe your experience with emergency protective orders, DVRO hearings, and safety planning for clients.

Tip: Show you understand both the legal process and the emotional weight.
How do you approach parental relocation disputes? ▼

Explain the legal standard for relocation, how you build or oppose a petition, and best-interests factors.

Tip: Relocation cases are emotionally charged and legally complex.
Describe your experience with guardianship and adoption matters. ▼

Discuss types of guardianships and adoption experience. Describe court process and home study coordination.

Tip: Breadth across these matters shows you can serve the full spectrum of family law clients.
How do you advise clients on post-decree modifications and enforcement? ▼

Describe your experience with motions to modify custody, support, or parenting time based on substantial change in circumstances.

Tip: Post-decree work is a steady revenue driver for family law firms.

Corporate / Transactional

What transactions have you worked on? ▼

M&A, PE, VC, general corporate. Specify deal sizes and your responsibility level.

Tip: Be honest about your role. Credibility matters.
How do you manage multiple closings? ▼

Closing checklists, signature pages, escrow coordination.

Tip: Show you are organized under pressure.
Walk me through negotiating a purchase agreement provision. ▼

Pick one you know: indemnification, reps, earnouts. Explain risk allocation.

Tip: Pick something you have actually negotiated.
How do you handle due diligence in M&A transactions? ▼

Describe how you organize and manage due diligence workstreams, coordinate with specialists, and identify material risks.

Tip: Due diligence efficiency separates strong transactional associates from average ones.
Describe your experience with representations, warranties, and indemnification provisions. ▼

Explain how you negotiate scope of reps and warranties, survival periods, materiality qualifiers, and indemnification caps.

Tip: R&W negotiation is core transactional work.
What is your experience with private equity or venture capital transactions? ▼

Describe the types of PE or VC transactions you have handled: LBOs, growth equity, recapitalizations, or early-stage financings.

Tip: PE and VC clients are demanding and repeat buyers of legal services.
How do you manage client communication and deal timelines under pressure? ▼

Describe how you keep deal teams aligned, manage expectations, and push deals to close.

Tip: Deals die from poor communication as often as from legal issues.

Paralegal

What types of cases have you supported? ▼

Drafting discovery, document review, depositions, motions, case calendars.

Tip: Be specific about volume and complexity.
How do you prioritize when multiple attorneys need help? ▼

Check deadlines, distinguish court dates from internal, communicate bandwidth.

Tip: Be proactive, not reactive.
What legal software are you proficient in? ▼

Westlaw, LexisNexis, Relativity, NetDocuments, Clio, iManage.

Tip: If unfamiliar with a tool, show you learn quickly.
Describe your experience drafting and responding to discovery. ▼

Walk through preparing interrogatories, requests for production, and responses. Describe how you organize document productions and apply privilege logs.

Tip: Discovery is where paralegals prove their value.
How have you supported attorneys during depositions and trial preparation? ▼

Describe scheduling depositions, preparing binders, summarizing transcripts, organizing exhibits, and coordinating logistics.

Tip: Paralegals who can run a trial prep room are extremely valuable.
How do you maintain accuracy when working on high-volume or repetitive tasks? ▼

Describe quality-control habits: checklists, double-verification, peer review, and systematic file organization.

Tip: Accuracy is the top trait firms look for in paralegals.
Tell me about a time you identified an issue an attorney had missed. ▼

Use STAR. Describe the situation, how you identified the issue, how you raised it professionally, and the outcome.

Tip: This tests both competence and professional judgment.

Legal Assistant

What administrative and legal tasks are you experienced with? ▼

Calendar management, correspondence, filing, client intake, expenses.

Tip: Show range across admin and substantive work.
How do you handle confidential information? ▼

Attorney-client privilege, secure handling, data security practices.

Tip: Mention locked workstations, encrypted email, secure shredding.
How do you support a busy attorney when unexpected urgent work comes in? ▼

Use STAR. Describe how you reprioritized, communicated impact on other deadlines, and ensured both urgent and standing work got completed.

Tip: Attorneys value assistants who adapt without drama.
Describe your experience with client intake and managing client communications. ▼

Describe how you handle initial client contact, gather information for new matters, and manage ongoing communication on behalf of attorneys.

Tip: Client intake sets the tone for the entire relationship.
What experience do you have with billing, timekeeping, and expense tracking? ▼

Describe entering attorney time, preparing draft invoices, tracking disbursements. Mention billing platforms you have used.

Tip: Billing accuracy directly affects firm revenue.
How do you handle situations where you are asked to do something you are unsure about? ▼

Describe your approach to asking clarifying questions, confirming instructions, and knowing when to escalate.

Tip: Legal work has zero tolerance for assumptions.

Legal Secretary

What document management systems do you know? ▼

iManage, NetDocuments, Worldox. TOA, TOC, blue-backing, court standards.

Tip: Formatting precision matters. Show court-specific knowledge.
How do you manage deadlines for multiple attorneys? ▼

Docketing approach, reminder systems, proactive confirmation.

Tip: A missed deadline can end a case. Show safeguards.
Describe your experience with court filing procedures and e-filing systems. ▼

Walk through your experience with local, state, and federal court filing requirements. Describe e-filing platforms you have used.

Tip: Court filing errors can have serious consequences.
How do you prepare and format legal documents for attorney review and court submission? ▼

Describe proficiency with TOA, TOC, blue-backing, exhibit tabs, signature blocks, and court-specific formatting rules.

Tip: Formatting precision is a core legal secretary skill.
How do you manage attorney travel, scheduling, and meeting coordination? ▼

Describe managing complex calendars, booking travel, scheduling across time zones, and preparing meeting materials.

Tip: Attorneys rely on their secretaries to own the calendar completely.
How do you handle communication with opposing counsel, courts, and clients? ▼

Describe drafting professional correspondence, managing incoming calls and emails, and handling sensitive information with discretion.

Tip: Legal secretaries are the front line of communication for a practice.

CPA Accountant

Walk me through your experience with audit engagements. ▼

Describe the types of audits you have performed (financial statement, compliance, SOC), your role on the engagement team, and the industries you have covered. Mention planning, fieldwork, and reporting phases.

Tip: Specify whether you worked on public (PCAOB) or private (AICPA) engagements. Partners want to know your exact level of responsibility.
How do you approach tax return preparation and review for complex clients? ▼

Describe your experience with individual, corporate, partnership, and trust returns. Discuss multi-state filings, estimated payments, extensions, and how you handle complex items like K-1 allocations or international reporting.

Tip: Mention specific forms (1120, 1065, 1040, 990) and software (UltraTax, GoSystem, Lacerte) to show depth.
Describe a time you identified a significant issue during an audit or tax engagement. ▼

Use STAR. Walk through what you found, how you investigated, how you escalated to the engagement partner, and the outcome for the client.

Tip: CPA firms value professionals who catch issues before they become problems. Show your technical instincts.
How do you stay current with changes in GAAP, tax law, and professional standards? ▼

Reference CPE requirements, firm-sponsored training, AICPA updates, IRS guidance, and how you apply new standards to client work in real time.

Tip: Show that continuing education is not just a checkbox but something you actively use to improve client service.
How do you manage multiple client deadlines during busy season? ▼

Describe your workflow management, prioritization system, and how you communicate with engagement managers about bandwidth. Discuss how you maintain quality under time pressure.

Tip: Busy season performance defines careers in public accounting. Show you thrive under pressure, not just survive.
What is your experience with accounting research and technical memo writing? ▼

Describe how you research ASC topics, draft technical memos documenting conclusions, and present findings to partners. Reference the codification, FASB updates, and any firm-specific research tools.

Tip: Technical writing skills separate senior-level CPAs from staff. Show you can document and defend a position.
Why are you looking to make a move, and what are you looking for in your next firm? ▼

Frame around growth opportunities, industry focus, firm culture, or path to partnership. Be specific about what you want and why this firm fits.

Tip: Never badmouth your current firm. Focus on what you are moving toward, not what you are leaving.

Accountant

Walk me through your month-end close process. ▼

Describe journal entries, accruals, reconciliations, variance analysis, and your role in meeting close deadlines. Mention how you improved close speed or accuracy.

Tip: Quantify your close timeline and any improvements you drove.
What accounting software and tools are you experienced with? ▼

QuickBooks, Sage, NetSuite, SAP, Oracle, advanced Excel (pivot tables, VLOOKUP, macros). Describe real projects where you used them.

Tip: If you have built automated reports or dashboards, highlight that. It differentiates you immediately.
Describe a time you found and corrected a significant accounting error. ▼

Use STAR. Walk through what you discovered, the financial impact, how you investigated the root cause, and what controls you recommended to prevent recurrence.

Tip: Errors happen. What matters is how you catch them, fix them, and prevent them from happening again.
How do you ensure accuracy and completeness in financial reporting? ▼

Describe your reconciliation process, multi-level reviews, tie-outs to source data, flux analysis, and how you handle discrepancies discovered late in the close.

Tip: Show a systematic approach to accuracy. Ad hoc quality control is a red flag.
How do you handle audit preparation and working with external auditors? ▼

Describe preparing workpapers, responding to PBC requests, managing auditor inquiries, and keeping the audit on schedule without disrupting daily operations.

Tip: Clean, well-organized workpapers make audits faster. Show that your documentation is always audit-ready.
What is your experience with accounts payable, accounts receivable, or payroll functions? ▼

Describe the volume and complexity of transactions you have managed, approval workflows, vendor management, collections processes, and any process improvements you implemented.

Tip: Operational accounting experience shows you understand the full cycle, not just GL entries.
How have you contributed to process improvement or automation in accounting? ▼

Describe a specific improvement: automating a reconciliation, eliminating a manual step, implementing a new tool, or redesigning a workflow. Quantify time saved or error reduction.

Tip: Candidates who proactively fix inefficiencies are the ones who advance fastest.

CPA Manager

How do you manage and develop your engagement teams? ▼

Describe how you delegate work, review staff output, provide feedback, and develop junior CPAs. Discuss how you balance coaching with meeting tight deadlines during busy season.

Tip: Partners promote managers who grow their teams, not just deliver engagements. Show you invest in people development.
Walk me through how you plan and manage an audit or tax engagement from start to finish. ▼

Describe your approach to engagement planning, risk assessment, staffing, timeline management, partner communication, and final delivery. Mention how you handle scope changes and budget overruns.

Tip: Managers own the engagement. Show you can run the entire process, not just execute individual sections.
How do you handle difficult conversations with clients about fees, deadlines, or findings? ▼

Describe a specific situation where you had to deliver unwelcome news to a client. Walk through how you prepared, communicated, and maintained the relationship.

Tip: Client management separates managers from seniors. Show you can have tough conversations professionally and retain the client.
Describe your experience with complex technical accounting or tax issues. ▼

Walk through a complex issue you resolved: revenue recognition, lease accounting, stock compensation, multi-state nexus, or international tax. Explain how you researched, documented, and presented your conclusion.

Tip: At the manager level, you are the technical expert the partner relies on. Show depth and confidence in your technical skills.
How do you contribute to business development and client retention? ▼

Discuss how you identify expansion opportunities within existing clients, participate in proposals, and build relationships that generate referrals and new work.

Tip: BD expectations increase significantly at the manager level. Show you are already building a book of business, even informally.
How do you ensure quality control across multiple concurrent engagements? ▼

Describe your review process, how you catch issues before partner review, and how you maintain consistency across different engagement teams and client types.

Tip: Quality control failures at the manager level reflect directly on you. Show you have a systematic review process, not just a final glance.
What is your path to partnership, and why does this firm fit that goal? ▼

Be specific about your timeline, the industry or service line focus you want to build, and how this firm's client base, culture, and growth trajectory align with your goals.

Tip: Firms hire managers expecting them to become partners. Show you have a clear, realistic plan and that this firm is the right place to execute it.

Controller

How do you structure and lead the month-end and year-end close process? ▼

Describe your close calendar, task assignments, review checkpoints, and how you drive the team to meet deadlines. Mention how you have shortened the close cycle or improved accuracy over time.

Tip: Controllers own the close. Quantify your timeline (e.g., reduced from 15 to 8 business days) and the team size you managed through the process.
Describe your experience building and managing an accounting team. ▼

Discuss hiring, training, performance management, and how you structure roles and responsibilities. Describe how you handle underperformers and develop high-potential staff.

Tip: Controllers are people managers first and accountants second. Show you can build a team, not just do the work yourself.
How do you ensure compliance with GAAP and internal controls? ▼

Describe the internal control framework you maintain, how you document and test controls, and how you prepare for external audits. Mention SOX compliance experience if applicable.

Tip: Controllers are the last line of defense on financial accuracy. Show you take internal controls seriously and have a documented framework.
Walk me through your budgeting and forecasting process. ▼

Describe how you build the annual budget, coordinate with department heads, produce rolling forecasts, and present variance analysis to the CFO or executive team.

Tip: Controllers who can forecast accurately are invaluable. Show you go beyond historical data and incorporate operational drivers into your models.
How have you implemented or upgraded an ERP or accounting system? ▼

Describe your role in system selection, implementation planning, data migration, user training, and post-go-live stabilization. Mention specific platforms (NetSuite, SAP, Sage Intacct, QuickBooks Enterprise).

Tip: System implementations are high-visibility projects. If you have led one, walk through the full lifecycle. If not, describe how you optimized an existing system.
How do you communicate financial results and recommendations to non-financial stakeholders? ▼

Describe how you translate accounting data into business insights, prepare board or executive presentations, and advise operational leaders on financial performance and decisions.

Tip: Controllers who can translate numbers into business language earn a seat at the leadership table. Show you are a business partner, not just a reporting function.
What attracts you to this company and this Controller role specifically? ▼

Research the company before your interview. Reference specific aspects: industry, growth stage, complexity, leadership team, or strategic initiatives that align with your experience and career goals.

Tip: Generic answers kill Controller interviews. Show you have done your homework and can articulate exactly why this is the right fit.

CFO

How do you approach strategic financial planning and capital allocation? ▼

Describe how you build long-range financial plans, evaluate capital investment decisions, manage debt and equity structures, and align financial strategy with business objectives.

Tip: CFOs are strategists first. Show you think in terms of enterprise value creation, not just financial reporting.
Describe your experience managing banking relationships, credit facilities, and treasury operations. ▼

Discuss how you manage lender relationships, negotiate credit terms, oversee cash management, and ensure adequate liquidity. Mention experience with revolver draws, covenant compliance, and interest rate management.

Tip: Cash is the lifeblood of a business. Show you manage it proactively, not reactively.
How have you supported or led M&A, fundraising, or other strategic transactions? ▼

Walk through your role in acquisitions, divestitures, debt raises, or equity financings. Describe due diligence, valuation work, deal structuring, and post-close integration from the finance perspective.

Tip: Transaction experience is a major differentiator for CFOs. Be specific about deal size, your role, and outcomes.
How do you build and lead a high-performing finance organization? ▼

Describe how you structure the finance function, hire and develop talent, set performance expectations, and create a culture of accountability and continuous improvement.

Tip: CEOs and boards hire CFOs who can build organizations, not just manage spreadsheets. Show your leadership philosophy and results.
How do you present financial performance and strategy to a board of directors? ▼

Describe your board reporting cadence, the metrics you present, how you frame strategic recommendations, and how you handle challenging questions from board members.

Tip: Board communication is a core CFO skill. Show you can distill complexity into clear, actionable insights and hold your ground under scrutiny.
Describe a time you identified and mitigated a significant financial or operational risk. ▼

Use STAR. Walk through the risk you identified, how you assessed the exposure, the mitigation strategy you implemented, and the outcome. Include both financial and operational dimensions.

Tip: Risk management is a defining CFO competency. Show you anticipate risks before they materialize and act decisively.
What is your vision for the finance function at this company, and how would you execute it in the first 90 days? ▼

Describe your assessment framework: reviewing financials, meeting with leadership, evaluating team capabilities, identifying quick wins, and building a strategic roadmap. Be specific about priorities.

Tip: A strong 90-day plan shows you are ready to lead from day one. Tailor it to the specific company and stage.

Describe building or overhauling compliance programs: risk assessments, policy drafting, training rollouts, and monitoring mechanisms.

Tip: Show you can build infrastructure, not just monitor it.
Describe a time you identified a compliance gap and how you remediated it. ▼

Use STAR. Walk through how you identified the gap, assessed severity, escalated, implemented corrective action, and validated the fix.

Tip: Show you have a systematic detection process, not just reactive problem-solving.
How do you conduct and manage regulatory examinations or internal audits? ▼

Describe coordinating with regulators or internal audit teams, preparing documentation, and responding to findings.

Tip: Show you run a professional, organized process that builds regulatory credibility.
How do you approach compliance training and building a culture of compliance? ▼

Discuss designing training programs, measuring effectiveness, and engaging employees beyond mandatory checkbox exercises.

Tip: Culture is the strongest compliance control.
How do you stay current with evolving regulations and assess their impact on the business? ▼

Reference monitoring sources: regulatory agencies, industry associations, legal updates. Describe translating new rules into actionable obligations.

Tip: Reactive compliance is table stakes. Show you see changes coming early.

The STAR Method

A proven framework for answering behavioral interview questions with clarity and impact.

S

Situation

Set the scene. Describe the context or challenge you faced.

T

Task

Explain your specific responsibility or objective.

A

Action

Detail the steps you personally took.

R

Result

Share the outcome. Quantify it if possible.

Example: Attorney Using STAR

S: A high-value client threatened to leave after a billing dispute during complex commercial litigation.
T: As the associate managing the account, I needed to resolve the dispute and retain the client.
A: I reviewed every line item with the client, prepared a revised summary, coordinated with the partner on a fair adjustment, and created a billing communication protocol.
R: The client stayed, approved the invoice within 48 hours, and expanded our engagement by two matters worth $200K+ annually.

Interview Tips

Actionable advice to help you walk in prepared and walk out confident.

🔍

Research the Employer

Know the firm's practice areas, recent wins, key clients, and culture. Check their website, LinkedIn, and recent news before your interview.

👔

Dress Professionally

Business professional is the standard for legal, financial, and engineering interviews. When in doubt, overdress.

💬

Prepare Your Stories

Have 5-7 STAR stories ready that highlight leadership, problem-solving, teamwork, and handling adversity.

✍

Prepare Smart Questions

Have 3-5 thoughtful questions about team structure, growth opportunities, what success looks like in 90 days, and the firm's direction.

🌎

Know Your Resume Cold

Be ready to speak fluently about every bullet on your resume. Interviewers follow up on specifics.

⏰

Arrive Early

10-15 minutes early in person. For virtual interviews, test your audio, video, and internet 30 minutes before.

🤔

Read the Room

Mirror the interviewer's energy. Strong emotional intelligence sets candidates apart.

📈

Quantify Everything

Whenever possible, use numbers. Numbers make stories memorable and credible.

📢

Follow Up Within 24 Hours

Send a personalized thank-you email to each interviewer. Reference something specific from the conversation.

🌟

Show Genuine Enthusiasm

Firms hire people who want to be there. Express specific excitement about the role, the team, or the firm's work.

🚫

Avoid These Mistakes

Never speak negatively about former employers. Never check your phone. Never give one-word answers. Never lie about your experience.

🤝

Negotiate Thoughtfully

When an offer comes, take 24-48 hours before responding. Research market rates. Counter professionally.
